My Energy Experience

Anxiety

Hailing from the sunshine state, my anxieties about climate change are uniquely tied to my geographical location. Florida especially is at risk from climate change due to sea level rise, extreme weather events, and extreme heat. Beyond that, social risks from gentrification continue to further endanger already at risk communities. While I was fortunate enough to grow up in an inland home with decent weather resistance, climate change endangers my family economically and interpersonally as the price of housing and home-upkeep in Florida rises and wages don't follow suit. Additionally, as both my parents and home age, it becomes more difficult to prepare the house for extreme weather events like hurricans. With the threat of sea-level rise as well, no property in the state is truly safe, as most of South Florida will eventually be threatened by extreme flooding. Beyond my state, I worry often about the existential effects of climate change. As a highschooler, I read plenty of articles for debate competitions about how climate change would spell the end of the world. These articles, whether exaggerated or not, had a lasting impact on how I view the modern climate crisis.


Anger

It's difficult to discuss my relationship with climate change without acknowledging the seriously messed up political situation of the state I grew up in. Despite being one of the most at-risk states in the country when it comes to climate change, Floridian politicians continually reject climate science and call mitigation-measures "liberal fear tactics." Additionally, the state continues to focus on producing and using natural gas and oil rather than facilitating a green-energy transition. Quite frankly, Florida is ****ed but politicians refuse to act due to hyper-partisan views and flagrant self-interest. Overall, politics is and always has been bought out by energy companies that see their economic growth as more valuable than our planet and its time for that to end. I'm angry that no matter what percent of Americans are in favor of energy change, or how bad the effects of cc seem to get, politicans refuse to change their agendas. Climate change is not a political matter, but its politicization is rampant nonetheless. As a citizen of Florida and of this country, I feel betrayed, ignored, and dejected by our political system.


Responsibility

Although much of the blame for climate change falls on energy companies and the politicians who support them, I recognize that I have an individual responsibility to do my part in creating the change I want to see. Coming from a state, zip code, and nation with an above average contribution to emissions, it falls on my to recognize my energy privelege and tackle it in ways that I can. According to the US Energy Information Administration, my home uses 270% of the average home energy use in the United States. After learning this number recently, I felt a new and profound sense of culpability for my emissions. For long, I had thought of the burden of climate change as being someone elses, perhaps the president or CEOs, but this information forced me to come to terms with the fact that my emissions are problematic as well. This begs the question, then, of what a mere individual can do to assist in the fight against climate change. This can fall anywhere from installing solar pannels, to making design changes like insulating windows, to reducing a/c usage during cooler days, to even plain old climate activism. With my energy-burden, I plan throughout the rest of my college experience to engage in climate activism through projects like this one that use simple web-development, self-reflection, and writing to discuss the climate crisis and its toll on our planet.
